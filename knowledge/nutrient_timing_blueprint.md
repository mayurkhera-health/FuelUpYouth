## Why Nutrient Timing Matters for Youth Athletes

Youth athletes possess faster metabolic rates and lower glycogen storage capacities than adults. This means they deplete fuel faster and must replenish it more consistently. A consistent, intentional stream of targeted nutrition prevents energy crashes, supports rapid physiological growth, and protects developing bodies from injury. Every eating window in FuelUp is derived from these evidence-based clinical guidelines.

## The Four Eating Windows

### Window 1 — The Gas Tank Meal (3–4 Hours Before Activity)

This is the foundational meal. Its purpose is to maximize muscle glycogen stores before physical activity begins.

**What to eat:**
- High complex carbohydrates (pasta, brown rice, whole-grain bread, oatmeal)
- Moderate lean protein (turkey, chicken, eggs, Greek yogurt)
- Low fat and low fiber to facilitate full gastric emptying

**Examples:** Turkey and cheese sandwich on whole-wheat bread with fruit; chicken breast with brown rice and steamed broccoli; scrambled eggs with whole-grain toast and a banana.

**Clinical basis:** Solid meals require 2–4 hours to clear the stomach. Exercising before gastric emptying is complete diverts blood flow away from skeletal muscles to the digestive system, causing cramping and performance decline (ACSM).

### Window 2 — The Top-Off Snack (30–60 Minutes Before Activity)

As the training or game window approaches, food choices must shift to avoid gastrointestinal distress.

**What to eat:**
- Simple, rapidly digestible carbohydrates only
- Minimal protein, near-zero fat or fiber

**Examples:** A banana, a handful of pretzels, graham crackers, applesauce pouches, toast with honey, rice cakes.

### Window 3 — During the Event (Depends on Duration)

Energy needs during exercise depend directly on duration and intensity.

**Under 60–75 minutes:** Plain water is sufficient. Exogenous carbohydrates provide no additional performance benefit for sessions under this threshold.

**Over 75 minutes (or extreme heat):** Introduce 30–60 grams of simple carbohydrates per hour to maintain blood glucose and motor coordination. Examples: orange slices, raisins, sports drinks diluted 50/50 with water.

**Tournament days (multiple games):** Electrolytes are mandatory. Sodium replacement is critical between games.

### Window 4 — The Recovery Window (Within 30 Minutes After Activity)

This is the most critical and most missed window. Insulin sensitivity is elevated immediately after exercise, creating optimal conditions for rapid glycogen resynthesis and muscle protein repair.

**What to eat:**
- 3:1 to 4:1 ratio of carbohydrates to protein
- Fluids to begin rehydration

**Examples:** Low-fat chocolate milk (the gold-standard clinical option), a turkey wrap, Greek yogurt with berries and honey.

**Clinical basis:** Combining carbohydrates with protein post-workout triggers a higher insulin response than carbohydrates alone. This opens muscle cells, accelerating glycogen storage replenishment while halting protein breakdown (IOC Consensus Statement 2015).

**Low-fat chocolate milk efficacy:** Randomized controlled trials validate chocolate milk as an exceptional recovery beverage because its natural composition aligns precisely with the required fluid, electrolyte, and macronutrient repair ratios.

## Hydration Schedule

Dehydration degrades athletic performance significantly before low blood sugar even manifests. Youth athletes frequently fail to recognize thirst cues until fluid deficits are already established.

| Timing Window | Fluid Target |
|---|---|
| 2–4 hours before exercise | 8–16 oz of water |
| During warm-ups | 8 oz of water |
| During exercise | 4–8 oz every 15 minutes |
| Post-exercise recovery | Sip water or low-fat chocolate milk freely |

## Core Rules — Never Break These

**Never skip breakfast.** Training in a fasted state forces growing bodies to utilize muscle tissue for fuel, hindering athletic progression and physical development.

**Prioritize micronutrients.** Adequate Calcium and Vitamin D for bone mineral density are non-negotiable during the peak bone mass window — this cannot be recovered later. Daily target: 1,300 mg calcium (AAP). Iron is critical for oxygen transport; 52% of female adolescent athletes are iron deficient — daily target 15 mg (Everett MD 2025).

**No adult supplements.** Pre-workout formulas, high-dose caffeine, and creatine are unnecessary and present safety risks to developing cardiovascular and renal systems. FuelUp never recommends supplements for under-18 athletes.

**Bedtime casein on heavy days.** On practice, game, tournament, or strength days, 30–40 g of casein protein before sleep (cottage cheese, Greek yogurt) supports overnight muscle repair — proven in adolescent athletes (Everett MD 2025).

## Application to FuelUp Eating Windows

Every meal slot generated by FuelUp maps directly to one of these clinical windows:

- **Breakfast / Gas Tank Meal** → Window 1 (3–4 hrs before first activity if morning event, or standalone if no morning event)
- **Pre-Game Fuel / Pre-Training Fuel** → Window 1 (3 hrs before event start time)
- **Power Snack** → Window 2 (45 min before event start time)
- **During Hydration** → Window 3 (water if event <75 min; carb + electrolytes if >75 min or tournament)
- **Halftime Fueling** → Window 3 mid-event top-up (games only)
- **Recovery Fuel** → Window 4 (within 30 min of event end — 3:1–4:1 carbs:protein)
- **After Training Recovery Fuel** → Window 4 variant for late-night events (event ends ≥ 7 PM). When practice or training ends at or after 7 PM, a full sit-down dinner is physiologically counterproductive — the body's priority is rapid glycogen resynthesis, not digestion of a large meal. FuelUp labels this slot "After Training Recovery Fuel" rather than "Recovery Dinner" to communicate that recovery nutrition (chocolate milk, Greek yogurt, a turkey wrap) is the correct choice, not a heavy plate. The nutritional targets are identical to standard Window 4.
- **Night Fuel / Evening Recovery** → Casein bedtime window (on active days)
